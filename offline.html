Hub · Net · Tide · Spin · Faker On-device, no servers

When the network drops, a small room opens.

Most apps show a sad cloud. Connect opens a hub. Four games take over: one to mend, one to breathe, two for a room full of friends sharing a phone.

The connectivity probe runs a four-second DNS check. If it fails, the hub appears. When the network is back, a quiet line fades in along the bottom. Tap it to return.

i.One player

Net.

Rotate to mend the network. An endless brain puzzle on a procedural grid, rooted in the Connect mark. Wider, twistier with every level.

ii.One player

Tide.

Breathe with a stranger. The resonance pacer, lifted out of the app into its own room. Five seconds in, five seconds out.

Open Tide
iii.2 to 10 in one room

Spin.

An editorial truth-or-dare. A circular dial of names, a hairline pointer at the top. Spin gives the wheel a five-to-seven second easeOutQuart deceleration. Each new slice ratchets under your thumb like a casino wheel.

iv.4 to 10 in one room

Faker.

A drawing imposter game. One player is secretly the faker. Everyone else gets the same word. The phone passes around: each player adds a single line. After the last line, the group talks it out and votes. If the faker is caught, they get one shot to guess the word and steal the win.

01.

The visual language matches across the hub.

Pure black canvas. A breathing dot-grid in the back. Playfair italic for the titles. Hairline strokes for the dividers. Editorial eyebrow labels and primary link buttons. No banners, no row chrome.

Hierarchy by type

Typography does the work.

The page is the container. A small Connect wordmark sits as a brand mark at the top. A short Playfair italic sets the mood. The games are typographic entries: ordinal, name in italic, description in italic, separated by hairlines.

No accounts, no servers

Everything runs on-device.

The hub doesn't talk to a backend. Net's grid is procedurally generated. Spin and Faker pass the phone, so the social state stays in the room. Tide is a local animation loop with a haptic on each phase.

02.

Net is the front door.

The grid hosts a procedurally generated network rooted at the centre tile, the Connect mark. Tiles start randomly rotated, so the network is broken. Tap to rotate ninety degrees.

Solve condition

Every stub joined, every tile reachable.

Connected tiles glow white. Disconnected tiles sit at twenty-eight percent. The source carries an inscribed Connect node and a slow halo breath. Solving advances to the next level: a wider grid, slightly twistier topology.

Endless. No fail. Best level persists locally.
Solve animation

A luminous wave radiates outward.

On solve, a wave fires from the source through the network in BFS order, then the next puzzle fades in. The brand and the mechanic are the same idea: things in their right place.

03.

Spin is the room game.

Names typed by the user during setup. Empty fields fall back to the editorial ordinal ("01", "02") so the wheel never has empty slices.

Two spins, then a prompt

The wheel chooses, twice.

One spin picks the asker. A second spin picks the answerer, biased away from the asker. The answerer taps Truth or Dare. The phone serves a prompt.

The wheel is the object

Not an arrow on a slice.

The dial is the moving part. A small hairline pointer notches into the top. Long, weighted deceleration on an easeOutQuart curve, five to seven seconds of suspense. Every new slice under the pointer fires a soft haptic ratchet.

04.

Faker is the drawing game.

For four to ten friends sharing one phone. One of you knows nothing.

Phases

Setup, rules, assign, pass, draw, discuss, vote, tally, guess.

The phone passes around the room. Each player gets the same word, except one. Two lines each on a shared canvas. After the last line, the group talks it out and votes on who's faking.

The faker's chance

One shot to steal the win.

If the faker is caught, they get a single guess at the word. Guess right and they win. Slip through the vote and they win outright.

A party game that runs on attention, not signal.
